1. Introduction: What We Mean by "Emerging"

This module is about emerging technologies. But what does that mean? Is it Generative AI? Virtual Reality? A library's first TikTok account?

The truth is, "emerging" is a moving target. What is "emerging" for one institution might be outdated for another.

The key concept is that technology doesn't emerge in a vacuum. It's a "confluence of advances", where multiple advancements (like faster networks, smartphones, streaming media, and AI) converge to create a broader impact.

This module will not focus on specific apps that could be gone next year. Instead, we will focus on durable frameworks for understanding, evaluating, and adapting to any new technology that comes your way.

2. Core Framework 1: The 5 Characteristics of Information Communities

We will use Fisher and Fulton's (2022) five characteristics of information communities to shift our focus from "what is this new gadget?" to "what does this new technology do for our communities?". This provides a theoretical framework that will outlast any specific app.

Click each characteristic to learn more:

1. Emphasis on Collaboration Among Diverse Information Providers

This characteristic highlights that collaboration is essential for gathering and distributing diverse information. Emerging technology acts as the "connective tissue".

  • Examples: Cloud-based platforms like Slack, Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and Discord have created the foundation for modern collaboration, allowing people to work together without being in the same building or time zone.
  • Technical Example: An Application Programming Interface (API) allows organizations to share data without sharing their systems. A museum's digital archive API could let a public library's website pull in relevant images for a local history project, creating a seamless user experience.
2. Capacity to Form Around People's Needs to Access and Use Information

Communities now form organically where the need is. They don't wait for an institution; they form a Facebook Group, Discord server, or Subreddit to solve a problem, share information, or advocate for a cause.

  • Example: Platforms like Nextdoor are information communities that form exclusively around a shared geographic need. The technology allows people to self-organize and share information at the neighborhood level.
3. Capacity to Exploit the Information-Sharing Qualities of Emerging Technologies

This is the core of this module. It's not just about sharing text; it's about sharing experiences, generating new content, and automating processes.

Exploiting the INTELLIGENT Web (AI)

Exploiting the IMMERSIVE Web (VR/AR)

We can now share experiences. A library or museum can offer a Virtual Reality (VR) tour of a historical site, allowing a user to "be there" in a way a book cannot. This technology fosters "social presence". An author talk in a VR social space allows attendees from around the world to feel like they are in the same room.

Exploiting the PARTICIPATORY Web (Social Platforms)

Information-sharing is no longer linear. Platforms like TikTok or Instagram Reels allow a single, compelling "how-to" video from a librarian to reach millions. We exploit this by inviting the community to share with us, such as asking for local stories or creating a "digital commons" where users upload and tag their own historical photos.

4. Ability to Transcend Barriers to Information Sharing

This is about access and equity. Technology can be a powerful force for breaking down walls that have traditionally firewalled information.

  • Language Barriers: AI-powered translation can translate text, audio, and even live conversation in real-time. AI can also simplify complex text (like legal or medical jargon) into plain language.
  • Accessibility Barriers: Technology is a game-changer for users with disabilities. AI-powered computer vision can describe images for the visually impaired, and advanced speech-to-text, text-to-speech, and voice control are now standard.
  • Cost & Format Barriers: Open Access (OA) and Open Educational Resources (OER) help eliminate paywalls. Projects like the Internet Archive provide access to rare items to anyone with an internet connection
